# Stardust: From the Big Bang to Your Hemoglobin

## Orion, Rigel, and the Mythology

In Greek mythology, Orion was a giant hunter — son of Poseidon, gifted with the ability
to walk on water. He boasted he could kill every animal on Earth, which provoked Gaia
(or in some versions, Artemis) to send a scorpion to slay him. Zeus placed both Orion
and the scorpion in the sky on opposite sides, so they would never meet again.

**Rigel** (Beta Orionis) marks Orion's left foot — the name comes from the Arabic
*Rijl Jauzā al Yusrā*, meaning "the left foot of the central one." It's a blue
supergiant at roughly 20 solar masses, about 860 light-years away, and one of the
most luminous stars visible to the naked eye (~120,000 times the Sun's luminosity).

Rigel is young — only about 8 million years old — and burning through its fuel at a
prodigious rate. It will exhaust its nuclear fuel and explode as a supernova within
the next few million years.

---

## The Orion Nebula and Heavy Element Seeding

The Orion Nebula (M42) sits in Orion's sword, about 1,350 light-years away. It's one
of the nearest active star-forming regions — a stellar nursery where new stars are
condensing out of gas and dust right now.

The connection to nucleosynthesis is direct: the gas in the Orion Nebula has been
enriched by previous generations of massive stars that lived and died in this region.
The Orion OB1 association (a group of hot, massive stars including those in Orion's
belt) has been producing supernovae for millions of years, seeding the surrounding
interstellar medium with heavy elements.

The new stars forming in the Orion Nebula today — and any planets that form around
them — will inherit this enriched composition. They'll have carbon, oxygen, nitrogen,
iron, and all the other elements needed for rocky planets and organic chemistry,
precisely because earlier massive stars in the same neighborhood manufactured and
dispersed those elements.

Rigel itself, when it eventually explodes, will contribute its own processed material
to the local interstellar medium — potentially seeding the next generation of star
formation in this region.

## Big Bang Nucleosynthesis: The Starting Point

The universe began with only the lightest elements:
- ~75% Hydrogen (by mass)
- ~25% Helium-4
- Traces of Deuterium, Helium-3, Lithium-7

That's it. No carbon, no oxygen, no iron. The nucleosynthesis window lasted only
about 3 minutes (from ~10 seconds to ~20 minutes after the Big Bang).

### The Mass-5 and Mass-8 Bottleneck

Why did it stop at lithium? Two gaps in nuclear stability:

- **No stable nucleus with mass 5.** He-4 + proton = Li-5, which decays in ~10⁻²² seconds.
- **No stable nucleus with mass 8.** He-4 + He-4 = Be-8, which decays in ~10⁻¹⁶ seconds.

You can't build heavier elements one nucleon at a time (blocked at 5), and you can't
fuse two helium nuclei (blocked at 8). The triple-alpha process (three He-4 → C-12)
requires sustained high density over long timescales — conditions available in stellar
cores but not in the rapidly expanding, cooling early universe.

---

## Stellar Nucleosynthesis: Building the Periodic Table

Stars bridge the gap. Once gravitational collapse creates cores hot and dense enough,
fusion proceeds through a sequence of burning stages.

### Minimum Stellar Mass for Each Stage

| Burning Stage | Minimum Mass (Solar) | Core Temperature |
|---------------|---------------------|-----------------|
| Hydrogen | ~0.08 | ~10 million K |
| Helium | ~0.5 | ~100 million K |
| Carbon | ~8 | ~600 million K |

Our Sun (1 solar mass) will burn H and He, then die as a white dwarf. Only stars
above ~8 solar masses reach carbon burning and beyond.

### Stellar Lifetimes (Main Sequence)

More massive = burns hotter = dies faster. Lifetime scales roughly as M⁻²·⁵.

| Mass (Solar) | H-Burning Lifetime |
|---|---|
| 1 | ~10 billion years |
| 5 | ~100 million years |
| 10 | ~20 million years |
| 20 (Rigel) | ~10 million years |
| 25 | ~7 million years |

## Rigel's Burning Sequence (20 Solar Masses)

| Stage | Fuel → Ash | Duration | Core Temp |
|-------|-----------|----------|-----------|
| H burning | H → He | ~10 million years | ~40 million K |
| He burning | He → C, O | ~1 million years | ~200 million K |
| C burning | C → Ne, Na, Mg | ~1,000 years | ~800 million K |
| Ne burning | Ne → O, Mg | ~1 year | ~1.5 billion K |
| O burning | O → Si, S, P, Ca | ~months | ~2 billion K |
| Si burning | Si → Ni-56 | ~1 day | ~3.5 billion K |
| Core collapse | — | seconds | — |

Each stage is dramatically shorter than the last. The fuel chain is:
**H → He → C → Ne → O → Si → Ni-56 (dead end)**

Each stage's primary ash becomes the next stage's fuel. Collateral products (P, S, Ca,
Na, Mg) accumulate but don't ignite further — they're ejected in the supernova.

### The Onion Shell Structure

At the moment of collapse, all burning stages are happening simultaneously at different
radii — the star has concentric shells like an onion, each burning a different fuel.

## The Supernova: Core Collapse and Explosion

### The Trigger

Silicon burning exhausts in ~1 day, leaving an inert iron/nickel core. Iron is the
end of the line — fusing past iron costs energy rather than releasing it. With no
energy source to provide pressure, the core collapses.

### Timeline of Collapse

| Phase | Duration | What Happens |
|-------|----------|--------------|
| Core collapse | ~0.1–0.5 seconds | Iron core free-falls to nuclear density |
| Bounce | milliseconds | Core stiffens into proto-neutron star |
| Shock stall | ~100–500 ms | Shock loses energy, stalls at ~100–200 km |
| Neutrino revival | ~0.5–1 second | Neutrinos re-energize the stalled shock |
| Explosion develops | seconds to minutes | Shock propagates through onion shells |
| Shock breakout | hours | First photons escape the stellar surface |
| Peak brightness | days to weeks | Powered by Ni-56 → Co-56 radioactive decay |
| Decline | weeks to months | Co-56 → Fe-56 decay powers the tail |
| Nebula visible | thousands of years | Expanding ejecta (like the Crab Nebula) |

### The Bounce Mechanism

The core collapses until it reaches nuclear density (~10¹⁴ g/cm³). At this point
neutron degeneracy pressure and the strong nuclear force make it incompressible.
The infalling material hits this "wall" — the newly-formed proto-neutron star — and
bounces.

### Neutrino Heating

The bounce alone isn't enough — the shock stalls. What revives it:

During collapse, protons absorb electrons (inverse beta decay):

    p + e⁻ → n + νₑ

Each conversion emits an electron neutrino. With ~10⁵⁷ protons converting, the
neutrino burst is enormous. The proto-neutron star is briefly opaque to neutrinos
(at nuclear densities, even neutrinos scatter). They diffuse outward over ~10 seconds.

A small fraction (~1%) of escaping neutrinos deposit energy in the "gain region"
behind the stalled shock during the first ~1 second. This is enough to revive the
explosion. The remaining neutrinos continue diffusing out as the neutron star cools.

SN 1987A confirmed this: neutrinos were detected over ~10 seconds, consistent with
diffusion from an opaque core.

### The Shock Wave

The revived shock starts fast (~0.1c near the core) but decelerates as it plows
through the massive stellar envelope — doing work photodissociating nuclei, ionizing
atoms, and accelerating mass. It takes hours to traverse the stellar surface.

## Nickel-56: The Iron Factory

Silicon burning produces **Ni-56** (28 protons, 28 neutrons), not Fe-56 directly.
Ni-56 is radioactive:

- Ni-56 → Co-56 (beta-plus decay, half-life ~6 days)
- Co-56 → Fe-56 (beta-plus decay, half-life ~77 days)

Each decay: proton → neutron + positron (e⁺) + neutrino (νₑ)

The positron annihilates with an electron, producing gamma rays. This radioactive
decay chain powers the visible light of the supernova for weeks after the explosion.

**The iron in your hemoglobin was briefly nickel inside a dying star.**

## What Requires Supernovae vs. What Doesn't

### Produced by stellar fusion (lighter than iron):

All top 10 body elements by mass:

| Element | Z | Source |
|---------|---|--------|
| Oxygen | 8 | He/C/Ne burning |
| Carbon | 6 | Triple-alpha |
| Hydrogen | 1 | Big Bang (primordial) |
| Nitrogen | 7 | CNO cycle |
| Calcium | 20 | O/Si burning |
| Phosphorus | 15 | O burning |
| Potassium | 19 | O burning |
| Sulfur | 16 | O burning |
| Sodium | 11 | C burning |
| Chlorine | 17 | O burning |

### Requires supernova/neutron star merger (heavier than iron):

- Iron (26) — at the boundary, produced as Ni-56 in Si burning
- Copper (29), Zinc (30) — trace elements in the body
- Iodine (53) — thyroid function
- Selenium (34), Molybdenum (42) — enzyme cofactors

These require:
- **r-process** (rapid neutron capture): neutron star mergers, some supernovae
- **s-process** (slow neutron capture): AGB star envelopes

## Dissemination: Getting Elements Into the Next Generation

Elements are forged inside stars but trapped there until the star dies.
Dissemination mechanisms:

1. **Core-collapse supernovae** — dominant source. Ejects all the O, C, N, Si, S, Ca, P
   from the onion shells. For a 20-solar-mass star: ~18 solar masses ejected (~90%).

2. **Stellar winds** (Wolf-Rayet, AGB stars) — massive stars shed material throughout
   their lives. Carbon and nitrogen especially.

3. **Type Ia supernovae** (white dwarf detonation) — major source of iron-peak elements.

4. **Neutron star mergers** — r-process elements (gold, platinum, uranium). Confirmed
   by gravitational wave observation (GW170817, 2017).

### How Many Generations?

Our solar system required ~2–3 generations of massive stars to reach its current
metallicity (~1.4% elements heavier than He). The first stars (Population III) formed
~200–400 million years after the Big Bang. Massive stars live only millions of years,
so there was ample time for multiple cycles before our solar system formed 4.6 billion
years ago.

## The Orion Connection

The Orion Nebula is this process happening in real time. Previous supernovae from the
Orion OB1 association enriched the local gas. New stars forming now in M42 inherit
that enrichment. Rigel, when it explodes in a few million years, will add its own
contribution — seeding the next generation.

We are made of stardust. More precisely: we are made of the processed, ejected,
recycled debris of multiple generations of massive stars that lived and died before
our Sun was born.

## Remnants: What's Left Behind

### Neutron Stars

For stars ~8–25 solar masses: the remnant is a neutron star (~1.5–2 solar masses,
~10 km radius). Supported by neutron degeneracy pressure. Can become a pulsar
(rotating magnetic field sweeps a beam like a lighthouse). Visible for millions of
years before spinning down.

### Black Holes

For stars above ~25–40 solar masses: the remnant exceeds the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff
limit (~2–2.5 solar masses). Even neutron degeneracy pressure can't hold it up.
Collapse continues to a black hole.

In some cases ("failed supernovae"), there may be no explosion at all — the star
simply vanishes.

### Rigel's Fate

At 20 solar masses, Rigel likely leaves a neutron star (remnant ~1.5–2 solar masses).
It will be visible as a pulsar for millions of years.

## Black Hole Jets: The Blandford-Znajek Mechanism

Spinning black holes can power relativistic jets (like M87's 5,000 light-year jet)
via the B-Z mechanism:

1. Accretion disk plasma generates magnetic fields (magnetorotational instability)
2. Field lines thread through the black hole's ergosphere
3. The black hole's spin twists them into helical corkscrews
4. Plasma is flung along the field lines as a collimated jet

This extracts rotational energy from the black hole, gradually spinning it down.
Up to ~29% of a black hole's mass-energy is stored as rotational energy and is
in principle extractable.

A black hole cannot become a "pulsar" — it has no surface to anchor a magnetic field
(no-hair theorem). Turn off the accretion and the jet stops. A neutron star pulsar
keeps pulsing in vacuum.

## The Numbers: You Are Stardust

For a 70 kg human:
- ~45 kg oxygen, ~12.5 kg carbon, ~7 kg hydrogen, ~2 kg nitrogen
- ~1 kg calcium, ~700g phosphorus
- ~4g iron (was briefly nickel inside a dying star)
- ~37 trillion cells, each with ~6.4 billion phosphorus atoms in DNA alone
- Total P in DNA: ~0.3 moles, ~9 grams

Every atom heavier than lithium in your body was manufactured inside a star and
ejected into space by a supernova, a stellar wind, or a neutron star merger.
